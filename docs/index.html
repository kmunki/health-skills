Usually quick and not a big deal—they numb the area with local anesthetic (a small pinch), then either shave off the top layer or punch out a tiny core. Takes a few minutes. The waiting for results (typically a week) is honestly the hard part.

You
I think it was CBC and metabolic panel. Maybe thyroid? I can check the patient portal.
Claude
Yeah, pull that up if you can—I can help you read it. "Normal" sometimes means "within range" but not optimal for you.

Things worth checking that basic panels miss:
• Vitamin D (very common deficiency, classic fatigue cause)
• B12 (especially if you're vegetarian or on certain meds)
• Ferritin (iron stores—can be low even with normal hemoglobin)
• More detailed thyroid (TSH alone can miss subclinical issues)

While you look: any other symptoms? Brain fog, muscle aches, mood changes, sleep quality, appetite, weight changes? Sometimes what seems like one thing is actually a pattern.
Understanding results Making sense of test data
You
Got my blood work back. Doctor said it's fine but some things are flagged. My vitamin D is 18 ng/mL (flagged low), TSH is 4.2 mIU/L (in range but high end), and ferritin is 22 ng/mL (in range). Does any of this explain why I'm so tired?
Claude
Yes, actually—this looks like a plausible explanation, even though only one is flagged.

Vitamin D at 18: This is legitimately low. Under 20 is deficiency, and many people don't feel well until they're 40-60+. This alone can cause significant fatigue, brain fog, and muscle weakness. Easy fix with supplementation.

Ferritin at 22: Technically "in range" but that range is wide. Many doctors consider under 30-50 suboptimal, especially for fatigue. If you're menstruating, this could be slowly draining your iron stores.

TSH at 4.2: Normal range goes up to ~4.5, but some people feel hypothyroid above 2.5-3. Worth watching, especially with the fatigue picture.

All three together? You've probably found your answer—or at least a big piece of it.

Worth asking your doctor about D supplementation (usually 2000-5000 IU daily to start) and whether they'd consider a trial of iron given your ferritin.

Good health advice needs context

Some people have clinical friends they can just text before paying for care. They're not getting "care" so much as advice and guidance—help thinking through what's going on and what to do about it.

AI is actually good at this. But most people use it wrong.

They present symptoms and expect answers. That's not how good health thinking works. What sets clinicians apart is they know that figuring out health issues requires identifying what isn't present as much as what is. This is why they ask weird questions about your family history, your meds, whether this hurts or that hurts. That process is called differential diagnosis—a structured way of ruling things in and out.

Example: You have a headache with a stiff neck. A clinician will almost instantly ask: "Can you touch your chin to your chest? Does it hurt when you do?" If you can, it rules out meningitis. Diagnosis is as much about what something isn't as what it is.

This is why WebMD always ends up at cancer—it can't help you rule out.

The trick: Give the AI context and let it know you're investigating together. Don't just present symptoms—let it ask you questions. Have a back-and-forth.

What a clinical friend knows about you

When you text a clinical friend, they already know things: your age, your general health, how anxious you tend to be, how you approach problems, what you care about. They know if you text them every other day or once every two years.

All of that nuance matters. Health care is weird—often all a clinician has to go on is what you tell them. So they're not just interpreting your symptoms, they're interpreting you: whether what you're saying is likely accurate, how you tend to describe things, what "a lot of pain" means coming from you specifically.

AI doesn't have this context unless you give it. This guide shows you how.

This isn't diagnosis. AI helps you think through possibilities, prepare for appointments, and understand what questions to ask. It doesn't replace a doctor who can actually examine you.

The basics

These practices work with any AI—Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini. No special tools required.

1. Sign in and stick with one AI

Context is key to good care. Pick an AI and sign in so it can remember you across conversations. You don't need a separate "health app"—those are more likely to be scams or have weaker models. Trust the big labs.

We recommend Claude because it's designed to engage substantively with hard questions rather than hedge everything. But ChatGPT and Gemini work too.

2. Use projects to keep context

Most AI apps let you create dedicated spaces that persist across conversations. Use one for health—it keeps your conversations together and lets the AI reference past context.

  • Claude Projects — best for this use case
  • ChatGPT Projects
  • Gemini Gems

3. Tell the story, don't just ask the question

"My foot hurts" gets you WebMD. "I rolled my ankle hiking three days ago, it swelled up that night, and it still hurts when I put weight on it" gets you actual thinking.

Talk to it like you'd talk to a friend who's a doctor. Include: when it started, what happened, how it's changed, what you've tried, what makes it better or worse.

4. Be honest about who you are

Your relationship with healthcare affects what advice is useful. Tell the AI:

  • "I tend to avoid doctors" — AI should be more direct about when to actually go
  • "I'm a bit of a hypochondriac" — AI should help calibrate worry appropriately
  • "I have health anxiety" — AI should be thoughtful about how it presents possibilities

Same symptom, different responses:

For someone who avoids doctors

"I know you tend to put off appointments, so I want to be direct: two weeks of this with no improvement is past the point of wait-and-see. This warrants a visit. Want help figuring out what to tell them?"

For someone with health anxiety

"Before we go down the list of possibilities—most of which are benign—let's focus on what's actually most likely given what you've described. The scary stuff is rare. Let's think through this calmly."

A suggestion to "see a doctor" means very different things to someone who goes at every twinge vs. someone who hasn't been in years.

5. Let it interview you

Don't just ask and accept the first answer. Good clinical thinking is iterative. If the AI doesn't ask follow-up questions, prompt it: "What else would help you think through this?" or "What questions would a doctor ask?"

The back-and-forth is where the value is. One-shot questions get one-shot answers.

Build your health context

The single most valuable thing you can do: spend 10-15 minutes giving the AI your health background. Do this once, save it to your project. Every future conversation benefits.

What to include

  • Basics: Age, sex, height, weight, where you live
  • Medical history: Current diagnoses, past surgeries, significant conditions (even resolved), allergies
  • Medications: Prescriptions with doses, regular OTC meds, supplements
  • Family history: Parents' and siblings' major conditions, patterns (heart disease, cancer, diabetes)
  • Lifestyle: Exercise, diet, sleep, alcohol/tobacco/cannabis, stress level
  • Your tendencies: Avoider or worrier? Natural remedy preference? How you like to receive information?

How to do it

Start a conversation in your health project. Say: "I want to give you my health background so you have context for future conversations. Can you interview me to build a complete picture?"

Let it guide you. Answer naturally—don't worry about medical terminology. When done, ask it to summarize what it learned and save that summary to your project's knowledge base or instructions.

Use your actual records. Most health systems let you download your medical records as a PDF from patient portals like MyChart. You can upload these directly to your health project for the most accurate context.

Privacy

Yes, you're sharing health information with an AI company. You can share as much or as little as you're comfortable with—even basic context helps. For Claude specifically, conversations are not used for training by default.

Know the limits

Not for emergencies. Chest pain, stroke symptoms (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty), breathing difficulty, severe bleeding, or anything that feels like "something is really wrong"—call 911 or go to the ER. Do not chat with AI.

What AI can't do

  • Examine you. A doctor can feel, press, listen, look closely. AI only has what you describe.
  • Run tests. Blood work, imaging, diagnostics require a healthcare system.
  • Diagnose. "Possibilities to consider" is different from "you have X."
  • Prescribe. Prescription medications require a licensed provider.

What AI can do

  • Help you think. Work through possibilities, consider what else to look for.
  • Prepare you for appointments. Clarify concerns, anticipate questions, prioritize.
  • Explain medical concepts. Help you understand conditions, tests, treatments.
  • Remember your context. Reference your history in every conversation.

The goal

Everyone deserves someone who'll actually think through their health with them.
