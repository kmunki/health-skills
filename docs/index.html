You
My ankle's been hurting for 3 days after I rolled it hiking. Still swollen, hurts to put weight on it. Should I see someone?
Claude
Three days of swelling and pain with weight-bearing after a rolled ankle—let's think through this. The fact that you can put some weight on it is actually reassuring (complete inability usually means something more serious). But persistent swelling and pain at day 3 suggests this isn't just a minor twist.

Possibilities to consider: Grade 1-2 sprain (most likely), small avulsion fracture (chip where a ligament pulled), or less commonly a hairline fracture. The Ottawa ankle rules doctors use: if you can take 4 steps and there's no bone tenderness at specific spots, fracture is unlikely.

What I'd tell a friend: This probably doesn't need the ER, but I'd get it looked at within a few days—urgent care or your PCP. An X-ray would rule out fracture and give you peace of mind. Until then: RICE (rest, ice, compression, elevation), NSAIDs if you can take them.

Why this works

Modern AI models know a lot of medicine. They've trained on textbooks, research papers, and clinical guidelines. But most people use them like a search engine—one-off questions with no context.

A doctor friend doesn't give you better advice because they know more medicine. They give you better advice because they know you: your history, your tendencies, how you relate to your health. That context changes everything.

The AI already has the medical knowledge. This guide teaches you how to give it the context it needs to use that knowledge well.

This isn't diagnosis. AI helps you think through possibilities, prepare for appointments, and understand what questions to ask. It doesn't replace a doctor who can actually examine you.

The basics

These practices work with any AI—Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini. No special tools required.

1. Sign in and stick with one AI

Context is key to good care. Pick an AI and sign in so it can remember you across conversations. You don't need a separate "health app"—those are more likely to be scams or have weaker models. Trust the big labs.

We recommend Claude because it's willing to engage substantively with health questions. But ChatGPT and Gemini work too.

2. Use projects to organize

Most AI apps let you create dedicated spaces that persist across conversations. Use one for health. This keeps your health conversations together and lets the AI reference past context.

Create a project called "Health" (or whatever you like). This becomes your dedicated space for all health conversations.

3. Be detailed—tell the story

"My foot hurts" gets you generic advice. The more context you give, the more specific the AI can be.

Instead of this
"My foot hurts, what should I do?"
Try this
"So the other day I was hiking and rolled my ankle on some rocks. It swelled up that night and I iced it. Now three days later it still hurts when I put weight on it, especially in the morning. The swelling went down but it's still puffy. I've been walking on it but it aches."

Tell the story like you would to a friend who happens to be a doctor. Include: when it started, what happened, how it's changed, what you've tried, what makes it better or worse.

4. Be honest about your biases

Your relationship with healthcare affects what advice is actually useful to you. Tell the AI:

  • "I tend to avoid doctors" — means AI should be more direct about when to actually go
  • "I'm a bit of a hypochondriac" — means AI should help calibrate worry appropriately
  • "I like to try home remedies first" — means AI should know your preference while flagging when that's not enough
  • "I have health anxiety" — means AI should be thoughtful about how it presents possibilities

This context changes how the AI should respond to you. A suggestion to "see a doctor" means different things to someone who goes at every twinge vs. someone who hasn't been in years.

5. Tell it what you already know

If you've already researched something, say so. If you have a theory, share it. If you're worried about something specific, name it.

Example
"I've been having these headaches for a few weeks. I've been reading about tension headaches vs migraines. I'm pretty sure it's tension-related because they feel like pressure around my forehead and get worse when I'm stressed. But I'm a little worried because my mom had high blood pressure. Can you help me think through this?"

Starting with what you know lets the AI build on your thinking rather than starting from scratch.

Building your health context

The single most valuable thing you can do: spend 10-15 minutes giving the AI your health background. Do this once, save it to your project. Every future conversation benefits.

What to include

Basic info

  • Age, sex, height, weight
  • Where you live (climate, altitude, relevant exposures)
  • Occupation (physical demands, stress level, exposures)

Medical history

  • Current diagnoses (diabetes, asthma, etc.)
  • Past surgeries or hospitalizations
  • Significant past conditions (even if resolved)
  • Allergies (medications, foods, environmental)

Current medications

  • Prescription medications with doses
  • Regular OTC medications (daily aspirin, antacids, etc.)
  • Supplements you take consistently

Family history

  • Parents' major health conditions
  • Siblings' major health conditions
  • Any patterns (heart disease, cancer, diabetes running in the family)

Lifestyle context

  • Exercise habits
  • Diet patterns (vegetarian, low-carb, etc.)
  • Sleep patterns
  • Alcohol, tobacco, cannabis use
  • Stress level and sources

Your healthcare personality

  • Your tendencies (avoider vs. worrier vs. proactive)
  • Your preferences (natural remedies, minimal intervention, etc.)
  • Your concerns or health anxieties
  • How you prefer to receive information (direct, gentle, detailed, high-level)

How to do this

Start a conversation in your health project

Say something like: "I want to give you my health background so you have context for future conversations. Can you ask me questions to build a complete picture?"

Let the AI guide you

Answer its questions naturally. Don't worry about medical terminology—just explain things as you understand them.

Save the summary

Ask the AI to summarize what it learned. Copy this into your project's instructions or knowledge base, so every new conversation starts with this context.

Use your actual records. Most health systems let you download your medical records as a PDF. Patient portals (MyChart, etc.) often have a "download records" option. You can upload these directly to your health project for the most accurate context.

Privacy considerations

Yes, you're sharing health information with an AI company. Consider:

  • Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini all have privacy policies. Read them if you're concerned.
  • You can share as much or as little as you're comfortable with. Even basic context helps.
  • For Claude specifically: conversations are not used for training by default. See Anthropic's privacy info.

Common scenarios

Different health concerns need different approaches.

Acute symptoms

"I woke up with X" / "This just started"

Focus: What is it, how severe, what to do right now. The AI should help you triage—urgent vs. can wait vs. home care.

Example prompt
"I woke up with a red, swollen eye. It's watery and a little crusty. No pain but it's itchy. Started this morning. Should I see someone today or can this wait?"

Ongoing mysteries

"This has been going on for weeks/months"

Focus: Pattern recognition, what's been tried, what would clarify. The AI should help you think systematically about possibilities.

Example prompt
"I've had this fatigue for about 2 months. Not tired-sleepy, more like no energy even after rest. Blood work came back normal according to my doctor. What else could this be? What should I ask about?"

Chronic conditions

"I'm managing X and trying to optimize"

Focus: Optimization, flare management, working with your care team. The AI should support your ongoing management, not try to solve it.

Example prompt
"I have Type 2 diabetes, managed with metformin. My A1C has been creeping up the last two tests (from 6.8 to 7.1). I haven't changed much. What questions should I be asking my doctor? What might explain the drift?"

Pre-visit prep

"I have an appointment coming up"

Focus: Clarifying your concerns, anticipating questions, prioritizing what to cover in limited time.

Example prompt
"I have a dermatology appointment Thursday for this mole I'm worried about. It's changed color over the past few months. What should I make sure to tell them? What will they probably want to know? What questions should I ask?"

Understanding results

"I got test results and don't understand them"

Focus: Explaining what the numbers mean, what's normal vs. concerning, what follow-up might be needed.

Example prompt
"I got blood work back and my doctor said everything's fine, but I see some things flagged as out of range. Can you help me understand what these mean? [paste results]"

Know the limits

Not for emergencies. Chest pain, stroke symptoms (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty), breathing difficulty, severe bleeding, or anything that feels like "something is really wrong"—call 911 or go to the ER. Do not chat with AI.

What AI can't do

  • Examine you. A doctor can feel, press, listen, look closely. AI only has what you describe.
  • Run tests. Blood work, imaging, and other diagnostics require a healthcare system.
  • Diagnose. "Possibilities to consider" is different from "you have X." Diagnosis requires examination and often testing.
  • Prescribe. Prescription medications require a licensed provider.
  • Handle emergencies. AI is not trained for emergency triage. When in doubt, call a human.

What AI can do

  • Help you think. Work through possibilities, consider what else to look for, understand trade-offs.
  • Prepare you for appointments. Clarify your concerns, anticipate questions, suggest what to mention.
  • Explain medical concepts. Help you understand conditions, tests, treatments, and options.
  • Provide a sounding board. Talk through decisions, weigh options, think out loud.
  • Remember your context. With proper setup, reference your history in every conversation.

The goal

AI helps you work with doctors better, not avoid them. It helps you show up prepared, ask better questions, and understand what you're told. It closes the gap between people who have clinical friends and people who don't.
