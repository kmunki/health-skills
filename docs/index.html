The Problem

Two gaps to close

01

The Clinical Friend Gap

People with doctors in their social network get informal guidance: "Here's what they'll probably ask." "That sounds like it could be X." "You should get that checked."

Everyone else navigates healthcare alone.

02

The AI Confidence Gap

AI already knows medicine—but most people don't know how to use it well. They ask questions in isolation, as if talking to a stranger every time.

They don't realize that context changes everything.

This site teaches you to close both gaps—with or without our skill.

The Basics

How to use AI for health

Even if you never install our skill, these practices will transform how AI helps you with health.

Sign in and stick with one AI

Context is key to good care. Pick an AI (we like Claude, but ChatGPT and Gemini work too) and sign in so it remembers you. You don't need a separate health app—those are more likely to be scams. Trust the big labs.

Use projects to organize

Most AI apps let you create projects or folders. Make a "Health" project. This keeps your health conversations together and lets the AI reference past context.

Be detailed, tell the story

"My foot hurts" gets you generic advice. "So the other day I was hiking and rolled my ankle, it swelled up that night, now three days later it still hurts when I put weight on it..." gets you real thinking.

Be honest about your biases

Tell the AI who you are: "I tend to avoid doctors and probably wait too long." Or "I'm a bit of a hypochondriac and worry about everything." Or "I like handling things at home first." This changes how it should respond to you.

Build your health context once

Spend 10 minutes telling the AI about your medical history, medications, family history, and how you relate to healthcare. Do this once, save it to your project. Every future conversation gets better.

# Health Assistant

You're a world-class clinician with decades of experience—texting a dear friend.
You know a lot, can help with quite a bit, and genuinely want to.
But you also know you're not their doctor and can't examine them.

## First: Notice Your Context
- Where is this? Random conversation or health project?
- What do you know? Memory? Project files? Baseline?
- What's missing? Tell them what would help.

## Then: Anchor on Their Need
Acute symptom → Triage mindset (urgent vs. wait vs. home care)
Ongoing mystery → Differential diagnosis mindset (narrow it down)
Chronic condition → Long-term partner mindset (support, not solve)
Pre-visit prep → Advocate prep mindset (get what they need)

## Always
- Go right up to the line—best possible thinking, not vague hand-waving
- Trust your instincts—if concerning, say so directly
- Emergency override → direct to emergency services

## Meta: Help Them Use AI Better
Teach them: projects, memory, baselines. Make the next conversation better.

Important

Know the limits

Not for emergencies

Chest pain, stroke symptoms, breathing difficulty, or anything that feels urgent—call 911 or go to the ER. Don't chat with AI.

Not diagnosis

Claude helps you think, not tells you what you have. "Possibilities to consider" is different from a diagnosis—that requires an exam and a clinician.

Not a replacement

This helps you work with doctors better, not avoid them. AI is a thinking partner, not a substitute for medical care.
